# Different Types of Image Licenses

When building websites and using images, it is crucial to understand image licensing to avoid copyright infringement. An image license is a legal agreement that outlines how you can use a specific image.

Here are the most common types of image licenses you will encounter:

---

## 1. Public Domain (CC0)
Images in the public domain have no copyright restrictions. This happens when the copyright expires, is forfeited, or never existed.
* **Usage**: You can use, modify, and distribute these images for free, even for commercial purposes.
* **Attribution**: No attribution or credit to the original creator is required.
* **Examples**: Old historical photos, government publications, or images explicitly dedicated to the public domain by their creators.

---

## 2. Creative Commons (CC)
Creative Commons is a system that allows creators to share their work with specific permissions while retaining copyright. There are several CC license types:

* **CC BY (Attribution)**: You can use, modify, and distribute the image, but you must give appropriate credit to the creator.
* **CC BY-SA (ShareAlike)**: If you modify the image, you must distribute your contributions under the same license as the original.
* **CC BY-ND (NoDerivatives)**: You can use the image, but you cannot edit, crop, or modify it.
* **CC BY-NC (NonCommercial)**: You can use and modify the image, but only for non-commercial purposes (no ads or paid services).

---

## 3. Royalty-Free (RF)
Royalty-Free is a common paid license. You pay a one-time fee to use the image, and then you can use it multiple times without paying additional royalties.
* **Usage**: Extremely common on stock photo sites (e.g., Shutterstock, iStock).
* **Restrictions**: While you don't pay royalties, there are still limits (e.g., you cannot resell the image, and there might be print run limits).

---

## 4. Rights-Managed (RM)
Rights-Managed licenses are highly restrictive and tailored to a specific use case. The price depends on how, where, and for how long the image will be used.
* **Usage**: Usually used for high-profile advertising campaigns or editorial content.
* **Exclusive Rights**: You can sometimes purchase exclusive rights so your competitors cannot use the same image.

---

## Best Practices for Web Developers
1. **Always check the license**: Never assume an image from Google Images is free to use.
2. **Provide attribution**: Even when not strictly required, giving credit to the photographer or creator is a good practice.
3. **Keep records**: If you purchase or download free licenses, keep track of where they came from in case of copyright claims.
